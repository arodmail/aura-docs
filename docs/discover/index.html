Discover

By Alex Rodriguez | Published August 31, 2021

The discovery step of the PAM on-boarding workflow begins with an assessment of a digital platform, that focuses on credentials to manage and the access requirements for an MSSP. This assessment explores the access needed, establishes a purpose for credentials, and examines a remote password change mechanism supported by the digital platform.

The discovery step completes 5 tasks:
  1. Test Environment
  2. Credentials Inventory
  3. Access Requirements
  4. Password Complexity
  5. Remote Password Change Mechanism

1. Test Environment

Gaining access to or setting up a test environment is a recommended first task to begin discovery. A test environment supports two discovery goals: to explore the digital platform's access control model, and to test a privileged access management approach in a working environment.

Any of the following assets may become a test environment:
  • Virtual Machine
  • Bare-metal server hardware
  • Cloud-hosted resource
  • Lab device
  • Small network of any of the above
  • Other
To support discovery goals, a test environment should be representative of a customer's digital platform to be managed by AURA.

Outcome of Task

  • Gain access to or setup a working digital platform instance for discovery and testing purposes.

2. Credentials Inventory

What elevated credentials are present on a host? In many cases, a superuser may exist, such as "root", "admin", or "administrator", which are special accounts used for system administration purposes. These are user accounts capable of making unrestricted, system-wide changes. Other, ordinary accounts granted less than superuser privileges, in some cases exist to run processes as functional accounts. This step in the assessment of a digital platform takes inventory of special and ordinary accounts already present, that need to come under management by AURA.

Outcome of Task

  • Discover and compile a list of credentials (usernames and passwords) that exist on the host, OS, application, device, or customer asset to manage.

3. Access Requirements

What elevated credentials are missing or need to be created? While pre-defined credentials for different purposes are often included out-of-box in a digital platform, application, OS, or on a device to be managed, additional credentials often need to be defined and added for different monitoring and administration purposes. For example, in order to administer a device in a basic way, SOC Analysts often share one or more sets of credentials with admin-level privileges.

An assessment of access requirements during discovery provides an understanding of the digital platform from the perspective of an MSSP: to manage and service a host, device, or other customer asset, what access to the digital platform is required? What credentials and permissions granted to them allow the MSSP to provide its services? For example, to perform authenticated health checks on a device, a dedicated functional account is created for this purpose and must be granted sufficient rights or privileges within the platform to login remotely and to perform a health check.

In addition to monitoring and administration purposes, accounts may be defined and added into the digital platform for other purposes such as:
  • Automation
  • Backups
  • Integration
  • Data acquisition
  • Auditing

A credential added into the digital platform to be managed by AURA, must belong to one of the following 4 categories:

 CategoryDescriptionRPC RequiredAuto ChangeCheck-Out EnabledCredentials Manager

Note: Check-Out Enabled: Yes, suggests use of the credential by a person because it gives a person exclusive access to (locks) the credential until Check-In. Check-Out Enabled: No is almost always reserved for use by an application, or headless access to a credential.

Outcome of Task
  • Create a list of credentials that are missing from the digital platform to manage.
  • Summarize privileges to grant to each credential (e.g., membership in a group) for automation or remote administration purposes.

4. Password Complexity

How strong are passwords in the digital platform? Because password strength is a requirement for most accounts, and the rules to generate a valid password may vary from one digital platform to another, discovery also gathers the requirements for account password complexity. Passwords should be strong enough to withstand a brute-force attack, such as a dictionary attack.

To defy attacks and to prevent guessing, strong passwords are based upon rules. This step in discovery provides an understanding of the rules that govern password complexity in the digital platform. Requirements for strong passwords include:

RequirementDescriptionMSS Standard
Minimum LengthMust be at least n charactersMust be at least 15 characters
Maximum LengthMust be at most n charactersMust be at most 26 characters
Character SetMust contain only lower case letters, upper case letters, numbers and symbols Categories: A through Z / a through z / 0 through 9 / ! $ # %

Password HistoryIf enforced, determines the number of unique new passwords that must be associated with a user account before an old password can be reusedNumber of passwords to store: 24

Outcome of Task

  • Create a summary of the rules that govern password complexity in the digital platform.
  • Assess whether or not MSS Standard password rules can be enforced.

5. Remote Password Change Mechanism

What is the remote password change mechanism supported by the digital platform? Most systems that recognize any form of remote login also provide a mechanism, a protocol, an API, or other means to change a password on an interactive login account remotely. For user accounts, changing passwords regularly (password rotation and expiration) provides assurances that if a password is ever disclosed to others, then it cannot be used for any length of time. PAM provides greater security by requiring passwords for shared accounts to change upon each use.

During discovery, the mechanism supported by the digital platform to change passwords remotely on privileged and ordinary accounts is examined and understood. Because change-password operations require that the user's current password be known before the change is allowed, discovery also takes into consideration how to obtain current password values from a credentials inventory.

Key questions to answer during discovery about an RPC mechanism:
  • What is the network protocol used by RPC? For example, SSH or REST?
  • What account attributes are required to perform a password change?
  • Does the platform support self-service password resets?
  • Are password changes only allowed to be performed by a "superuser" account?
Outcome of Task
  • Answer key questions
  • Successfully perform a remote password change on an account
  • Take notes on the RPC mechanism, use the test environment, capture the commands executed or the APIs called

6. Discovery Summary

The following outcomes are expected from discovery tasks:
  1. Gain access to or setup a working digital platform instance for PAM tests
  2. Compile a list of credentials (usernames and passwords) that exist in the digital platform.
  3. Create a list of credentials (usernames and account types) that are missing from the digital platform based upon access requirements.
  4. Create a summary of the rules that govern password complexity in the digital platform.
  5. Answer key questions about an RPC mechanism supported by the digital platform, and successfully perform a remote password change on an account.

7. Next Step

Once discovery is complete, PAM designers may move to the next step in the PAM on-boarding workflow: Design.
